Journal

With a Nor’easter setting its sights on New York and promising a proper dumping of snow, my mind wandered to the days of dry trail and soft crunch of leaves underfoot. Because of the storm, a half-planned trip on the Appalachian Trail to finish New Jersey was now impossible without the aid of snowshoes and a spider web of logistics spanning Pennsylvania, New Jersey, and New York. Feeling a bit down, I talked to my fiancé about potential options for the next week and low-and-behold we concocted a plan to flee south. Within 12 hours we had made the decision to make a mad dash to the rolling hills of Virginia.

The car was loaded with all the necessary items to work for the week, maintain our fitness via kettlebells and dumbbells, and somebody (not naming names) threw in some backpacking gear into the trunk as well. Once the final items were stashed away, we got a move on to avoid any potential issues with the snowstorm nipping at our heels. The drive was straightforward and nothing out of the ordinary other than the highway signs warning of the upcoming blizzard conditions. But it didn’t matter, we beat the storm and were now in Virginia. There, the waiting game began. Forecasts were swinging wildly and there wasn’t any sort of clarity as some metrologists were calling for 1 inch of snow while others were saying up to 2 feet of snow were due to fall on Virginia soil.

We were greeted the following morning with a dreary, grey sky with thick, wet flakes of snow slowly dropping from above. Throughout the morning, the snow had a difficult time sticking to anything as the temperatures were hovering just above freezing. As the day wore on, the snow slowly turned to a soaking rain, wiping out any snow that did manage to hang around through the morning. Daylight eventually receded with no snow on the ground, but as night fell, the temperatures dropped and the snow began once again. The true damage would have to be seen the following morning. Nervous to check on the damage, I climbed out of bed and pulled back the blinds only to discover half an inch of snow. A total dud of a storm in Marshall, Virginia.

Now, this would’ve been a horrific opening story if there wasn’t another side to this. Marshall, Virginia sits below 700 feet above sea level. I’m no weather expert, but I do know that as you go higher in elevation temperatures drop. And remember when I said that temperatures were hovering at right around freezing the prior day? Well, up in Shenandoah National Park, all of that rain was actually snow. My mad dash south to avoid the snow had hit a snag.

This may come as a shock to some of you, but I have a tendency to fixate on things. And boy, did I fixate on the snow levels in Shenandoah National Park over the next five days. My internet search history was littered with National Park Service webcams, traffic cams, meteorology reports, and mathematical equations for the rate of snowmelt per day, etc. Is it embarrassing to admit this? Yes. Did it help with planning? No. After spending the work week constantly thinking about how much snow there would be on top of Hogback Mountain, I came to the shocking conclusion that it didn’t matter because I was going to walk through it anyway. Armed with the useless knowledge that there had been a fair bit of snowmelt over the past few days at the intersection of Skyline Drive and US Route 211, I packed my gear and hit the road.

I arrived at the Mountain Home B&B, just outside of Front Royal, Virginia, on Friday afternoon. After some small talk with the owner of the hostel, I gave her the car keys and made my way south on the Appalachian Trail towards Shenandoah National Park. Being just 10 months removed from a trip on this exact section, I had fresh memories to draw on as I ascended to the northern boundary of Shenandoah National Park. I looked at the sides of the trail and remembered the Virginia Bluebells that were just sprouting and the first hints of green returning to the world. This time, I was too early. Still over a month away from life returning to the hills rising above Front Royal. Despite the lack of greenery around me, the evening delivered springtime temperatures and for the first time in quite a while I had bare hands gripping the cork on the trekking poles. About an hour and a half after leaving the hostel, I arrived at the base of a rocky outcropping that I knew served as the northern terminus of the Appalachian Trail through Shenandoah National Park. Life perfectly coalesced in this moment as the sun slowly faded from view and lit up the sky the second that I reached the park boundary. With the sun gone for the day, the remainder of miles would be under the night sky as darkness took hold.

I used to be afraid of the dark. And this absolutely continued into adulthood. For example, I remember being in St. Simons Island, Georgia one night in 2023 under a new moon and my anxiety was a 9/10 just walking through the gated neighborhood in the dark. Suffice to say, night hiking was not a natural fit for me initially. In fact, my first forays into the backcountry were very uncomfortable because of the nights. Slowly but surely, I ratcheted up the intensity over the years while living in Cold Spring to the point where it now feels normal, even comfortable. I guess exposure therapy is a viable solution after all. Snapping back to Virginia, the daylight slowly faded from view making the monochromatic winter world even more dull. About 45 minutes after sunset, I was still walking without the assistance of a headlamp when a funny thing happened. I looked down and saw my shadow. My obsession with snow levels in the lead up to the trip meant that I totally ignored the moon phase, which at the time was nearly full. This combined with a canopy bare of leaves meant that travel by moonlight was not only possible, but easy.

My shadow sharpened as the daylight exited the stage and the moon rose in the night sky. The experience of quietly moving through the woods in the dark was empowering. Just a few years removed from visceral fear gripping me walking in a gated neighborhood in the dark, I was alone deep in the mountains with a smile on my face. In that moment, very little separated me from the animals that called that forest home. I walked for about two and a half hours without the use of a headlamp and eventually arrived at the point where it needed to come on. Why? Remember my talk earlier about how it gets colder as you go higher? Well, I found the snowline and navigating snowy and icy terrain in the dark seems like a really dumb way to get injured. Armed with an artificial sun on my forehead I climbed higher into the park.

A lot of the walking on the first night of the trip is in a sort of “no-mans-land” where there aren’t any notable mountains or climbs, rather just forested ridges. That section comes to an end with North Mt. Marshall, the last 3,000 ft. mountain on the Appalachian Trail until northern Massachusetts. My first time on this mountain was just after sunrise with the whole world illuminating below me. This time, it was only the stars and moon above with the lights from the surrounding valleys reminding me “real-life” wasn’t too far away. After going up and over Mt. Marshall, the only thing left for night one was finding a place to camp. I continued on for a few miles and arrived at Gravel Springs Hut, a shelter that I stayed at in 2025, around 9:15pm. There, I pitched my tarp and climbed into the sleeping bag after knocking out 14.2 miles and 3,697 feet of elevation gain.

Morning arrived with a heavy layer of condensation blanketing the tarp. I packed camp, did some quick chores, and hit the trail heading southbound. Similar to my Georgia trip just a few months prior, Saturday was the crux of the trip. It was the only day where mileage was open-ended, and I could be as flexible as I wanted. That meant I had no set plans leaving camp, the day was fully mine to craft. With that in mind, the earlier part of the day was going to include the sections of the park that I really enjoyed my first time through, so it seemed like a nice idea to walk through it in the morning and the evening. Great. That meant that the tentative plan was a longer day and with the existing snowline being a common companion throughout the day, I knew that there was potential for slower travel. Well, that slower travel hit hard and fast as I found myself postholing up to my knees while on top of Hogback Mountain. My plans came crashing down in my head as I was breaking trail and travelling at roughly 1.5 miles per hour. While on Hogback Mountain, I scaled back my plans and was ready to admit defeat by capping the day at 20 miles. I’d decided to travel 10 miles and turn around so that I could get back to Gravel Springs Hut for the night. Once I made that decision, my mood lightened and instead of being pissed off by the deep snow, I started enjoying the day. I focused on the songbirds chirping to their hearts’ delight, the streams flowing with fresh snowmelt, and the warmth of the sun on my face. It made all of the other problems melt away, I didn’t care that my feet were wet or that my microspikes were on, rather I stepped into a healthier, happier headspace where I was just glad to be outside.

Then, something strange happened. The deep snow never reappeared. I’m not sure if Hogback Mountain got triple the amount of snow as other parts of the park, but the rest of the day was spent walking through slushy, melting snow or dry ground. My travel speed picked up to over 3 miles per hour once again and the 20-mile self-imposed limit was removed. I was free to go about the day however I saw fit. In that moment, it meant hitting the gas. One of my goals for this trip was to get to Mary’s Rock, a notable peak in Shenandoah National Park known for its western facing view of the Shenandoah Valley. Thing is, that meant that it would turn this weekend getaway into a 60-mile trip. I gave myself the grace to make a call when I got deeper into the day. And before getting too much further into that, the morning was fantastic. I found myself walking on ridgelines with gorgeous views of the surrounding mountains and valleys as the crisp morning air gave way to a sunny 60 degree day.

Right around midday, I arrived at Thornton Gap, which sits at the base of Mary’s Rock. Before I get into the decision that I had to make, here’s some important context. My fiancé was kind enough to stay back and do wedding planning while I was out doing this trip. This meant that I was cognizant of the fact that I shouldn’t be out too late on Sunday. The further that I went into the park on Saturday meant the more miles I’d have to cover either later that day or Sunday morning. So, with that in mind, I turned off airplane mode to check in with her and see if there were any updates. Lo and behold, there was an important one. Part of the reason that we travelled to Virginia was for a tennis tournament hosted by a club that my partner’s family grew up attending. My fiancé’s late father was a fantastic tennis player, a vibrant member of the local community, and an extremely likeable man despite his fiercely competitive nature. Following his death in 2021, the club decided that it’d be best to give out an award in his honor at the end of the competition every year. So, my fiancé and her mother wanted to be there for the award ceremony. This presentation was supposed to be on Saturday; however, I got the news at Thornton Gap that it had been moved to Sunday at noon.

There I was, in a grassy patch right beside the tolling both on Skyline Drive, unshod with my gear drying in the midday sun when I made the decision to forgo the climb up to Mary’s Rock and turn around exactly where I was so that I could be there for my partner. That meant I wasn’t going to achieve my stretch goal, that I was going to finish ahead of when I wanted to, and that I had to manage the logistics of not being a stinky mess upon arrival at the club. But it didn’t matter, she was kind enough to let me do something I needed so the least I could do was show up when she needed me there.

I packed up my bag and started back covering the same trail I had just traversed. The warm day had accelerated the snowmelt and with more dry ground underfoot, my mind was free to go where it pleased. I found myself back in my fourth-grade classroom under the tutelage of Ms. Thompson, a phenomenal educator that I place at the top of the list for my favorite teachers. Some of my most profound memories in my entire education come from my time spent in the trailer on the blacktop of East Side Elementary. In one assignment, I built our solar system with foam balls and spray paint; in another, I created a replica Yellowstone National Park out of a shoebox, complete with a smoking geyser and all; in another, I was dressed as Leif Erikson giving speeches to my fellow classmates about the Vikings and their landing in Iceland. But one assignment has stuck with me more than any other over the years, a simple one too. Write a short story from the perspective of a pair of shoes. That was it. Pick a pair of shoes and write about them. At the time, I didn’t fully understand what Ms. Thompson was trying to teach and the story that I wrote was probably pretty simple. If I had to bet, she was trying to teach us many lessons, with the most important being gratitude, awareness, and empathy. Being grateful for the fact that you had shoes to write about, being aware of what your shoes might say about you, and being able to pull your perspective away from yourself and shift that awareness to something else.

I looked down at my shoes. Battered. Absolutely put through the ringer. They had been a part of my journey for over 600 miles spanning the Colorado Trail, icy mornings in the Hudson Highlands, beach runs in coastal Georgia, and everything in between. I have a complicated history with these shoes, and it is obvious why with the slightest bit of inspection. The super glue spill on the bottom speaks to the resuscitation efforts to get the outsole reattached and in workable condition, the holes and friction marks on the insoles tell stories of blisters past and present, the fraying fabric on the upper mesh tell how these shoes were relegated to “winter beater” shoes that got chewed up by microspikes. You might look and see a beat down pair of shoes that should have been thrown away months ago, but I see small threads of experiences that tie together to create formative experiences in my life. I think if I were to go back and complete that assignment from fourth grade today, I’d write about the complicated, symbiotic relationship that these shoes and I share and the beauty that is derived from care and experience.

At some point, I removed myself from the vortex of philosophical thoughts about my shoes and returned to the task at hand. The shadows began growing longer as I arrived back at Hogback Mountain and in an unbelievable turn of events, a full bootpack had beat down the snow in that section and travel was fairly seamless. It was a late February miracle. I pressed as sunset approached.

Because the Appalachian Trail in Shenandoah National Park runs on several ridgeline throughout most of the park, water is not the easiest thing to find on trail. Often, hikers have to descend to a spring to fill up before climbing back to trail to continue. That is exactly the situation I found myself in around 5pm that night. Gravel Spring Hut was just below and the promise of flowing water and a privy drew me down. In what can only be dubbed as a hit-and-run, I abused the Gravel Springs Hut privy before filling up my water and carrying on. Climbing back up to trail, I did some quick mental math and determined that if I climbed hard I could reach the top of North Mt. Marshall at the exact moment that the sun would be setting. After 28 or 29 miles already under my belt, I clicked into a new gear and began the race to the top. The climb is nothing too special or difficult and before long I found myself on a cliff outcropping with a perfect west-facing view.

I have been fortunate to experience some incredible sunsets and sunrises in my life, but for some reason the ones that stand out the most are those that require the most effort to achieve. 30 miles into the day, I sat perched atop the tallest mountain for hundreds of miles on this trail, with the Blue Ridge Mountains sprawled out in front of me with the sky flashing hues of blue and orange in every direction. As is typical in these scenarios, I got pretty emotional. Tears didn’t come out but I was simply overcome with a sense of gratitude. Doing these trips, I often get caught in the trap of constant forward progress, but it’s often the small moments where I stop and take in the view that are most memorable. I sat there for a while. I knew that I would have to find camp in the dark. I knew that my body was tired and that the sooner I got in my quilt the better. But it didn’t matter. The only thing that did matter was that I was right where I needed to be experiencing a moment that will only come once.

The descent from the top of North Mt. Marshall was simple and I found a flat site about a mile or two from the top of the mountain. I pitched my tarp and called it a night. The day totaled 32.1 miles and 6,647 feet of elevation gain in eleven and a half hours of moving time.

Because of the obligations that I had to attend at noon, I knew that I had to knock out the remaining miles fairly early on Sunday. At 5:30am I was greeted to the living world by my beeping watch. When I sat up, I noticed something strange, a bright red bulb just under the ridgeline of my tarp. At first, I rubbed my eyes and tried to knock the gunk out of my brain as I thought it was the sun, but the synapses started firing and let me know that I was actually looking at a Blood Moon. Under the watchful eye of the Blood Moon hanging on to the horizon, I packed my gear and started down the trail at around 6am.

It sounds annoying and cocky to say this, but the 10.5 miles for the day was more or less a formality. I was familiar with the terrain, which was mostly downhill and nondescript, and didn’t need to expend a lot of effort to get the trip buttoned up. What this morning did give me was the time to think about the takeaways from the trip and how I’d carry the lessons forward.

The first and most obvious takeaway was that the gear that I had made this winter had performed wonderfully. I took the MYOG pack and tarp on this trip, both for the first time, as a test for the upcoming hiking season. The pack carried like a dream and often times I forgot that it was even there. I went into the trip wondering about adding additional pockets to the pack; however, I arrived at the finish thinking that there weren’t any necessary adjustments. The tarp also performed well, despite not being fully tested by the rain. After learning the best ways to pitch on night one in the dark, I used those learnings on night two and got to live the palace-esque experience under the tarp. There are a few additions that I want to make to the tarp, like linelocs and a clothesline, but it passed with flying colors. All of the other gear and apparel held up well despite temperatures being in the low 30s overnight.

The second takeaway was that these short weekend hikes are a great way to get the adventure that I need without blowing up my life. For a while, I thought that it was long hikes or bust and that these smaller hikes simply weren’t enough. But after back-to-back high mileage weekend trips, it proves the opposite stance is correct in that any time spent outside is enough for me.

The final takeaway is that I really handle the duality of my life fairly well. I can go from looking extremely homeless at Thornton Gap on Saturday at noon to being a buttoned up member of functioning society at a tennis club awards ceremony on Sunday at noon. I can balance both sides of my life without fully having to choose one or the other.

As these thoughts were racing through my head, I found myself at the park boundary once more and knew that I only had an hour left of walking to go. As the last of the protein bars went down the hatch, the end came into view and before long I was knocking on the hostel door to get my car keys and make my way to the civilized world.

I got back to a quiet house where I quickly showered and changed before making my way to the Middleburg Tennis Club. There I was just another person going about their day, with no signs of 57 miles of walking on me at all. I talked with new and familiar faces about anything that wasn’t backpacking related before the attention of the room centered on my fiancé and her mother. I stood across the room watching her put on a brave face as she handed out the award bearing her father’s name. It was worth being there.

Everyday I am searching for ways to find balance between these extremes. For this weekend, I think I threaded the needle perfectly.
